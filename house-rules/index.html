Introduction

This document explains the variant rules, optional rules, and house rules I use when running Dungeons & Dragons 5e. The document assumes the Player's Handbook as a baseline, but may also refer to other rules texts published by Wizards of the Coast.

The Rules and Options table lists each rule in this document as either an option or a rule; players who aren't interested in additional flexibility can safely ignore those marked as options, but every player should know the rules.

Recent Wins

One general rule applies to all of the following: when a character option, spell, or other piece of rules text appears in multiple sourcebooks, the most recently published version is the one you should use. In most cases the text is the same, but in case of a discrepancy the more recent text is correct.

Character Creation and Advancement

The following rules supercede or supplement the rules for step-by-step character creation in chapter one of the Player's Handbook. The house rules here are designed to make character creation possible with no dice rolls (and hence no DM oversight), as well as to provide flexibility for players to build the character they want to play.

Subraces and Variants

For each of the races listed in the table above, you may use any variant or subrace in any of these sources:

  • Player's Handbook
  • Elemental Evil Player's Companion
  • Sword Coast Adventurer's Guide
  • Volo's Guide to Monsters
  • The Tortle Package
  • Mordenkainen's Tome of Foes
  • Eberron: Rising from the Last War

This includes subraces from texts other than the one that introduced the race. For example, gnomes are introduced in the Player's Handbook, but you are free to choose the deep gnome subrace from Mordenkainen's Tome of Foes. Similarly, variant half-elves as described in the Sword Coast Adventurer's Guide are allowed, as are dragonmarked variants described in Eberron: Rising From the Last War.

Flexible Ability Score Increase

Want to play a duergar bard, a halfling barbarian, or an aasimar wizard? Some players feel restricted in their creativity by the "need" to have a +3 modifier in their primary ability score.

If your character's race grants an Ability Score Increase feature with a +2 to any ability score, you may choose to give up one +2 increase in exchange for a +1 increase to one other ability score. The ability score you increase in this way cannot be one that your race or subrace already modifies. Characters with more than one +2 increase, such as mountain dwarves, can only make this swap once.

For example, if you select lightfoot halfling, your Ability Score Increase feature grants +2 to Dexterity and +1 to Charisma. You could exchange the +2 to Dexterity for a +1 to Strength, Constitution, Intelligence, or Wisdom.

No Rolling for Ability Scores

The Player's Handbook contains rules for rolling dice to randomly generate your character's ability scores. On page 13, it also presents a variant rule for customizing ability scores by purchasing them with points instead of rolling randomly. This variant point-buy method is required.

Artificers Allowed

When choosing a class, you may choose the artificer class from Eberron: Rising From the Last War.

No Alignment

Dungeons & Dragons 5e has essentially done away with alignment as a game mechanic. Spells like detect good and evil don't refer to alignment except in the name. This rule makes it official: there is no such thing as alignment. You can describe yourself as "chaotic good" if you like, but it won't matter except as a role-playing guide. You are not required to choose an alignmet during character creation.

Average Starting Wealth

The Player's Handbook presents two options for determining your characters starting equipment. You may either choose the package granted by your class and background, or purchase equipment from the tables in chapter 5. If you take the latter option, the amount you have to spend is shown on the Starting Wealth by Class table, which supercedes the table of the same name in the Player's Handbook.

Starting equipment for levels 2–4 is the same as at level 1. Starting equipment beyond level 4 will be determined on a case-by-case basis.

Average Hit Points

When you gain a level, the Player's Handbook directs you to roll your new Hit Die to determine how much your hit point maximum increases by. Instead, you must use the alternative rule: your hit point maximum increases by the fixed value shown in your class entry, which is the average result of a Hit Die roll (rounded up).

Multiclassing Allowed

Chapter 6 of the Player's Handbook describes optional multiclassing rules. You are allowed to multiclass using these rules, subject to DM veto. Inform your DM of your multiclassing plans before leveling up.

Feats Allowed

Chapter 6 of the Player's Handbook describes an optional rule that permits trading an Ability Score Adjustment class feature for one of the feats listed in that chapter. This optional rule is in effect.

The list of feats is also expanded. Feats from other official sourcebooks are allowed, including but not limited to the racial feats from Xanathar's Guide to Everything, the optional Deep Gnome Magic feat from Mordenkainen's Tome of Foes, and the Aberrant Dragonmark and Revenant Blade feats from Eberron: Rising from the Last War.

Expanded Spell Lists

Your class's spell list includes all the spells on that list from the Player's Handbook as well as those added by Sword Coast Adventurer's Guide and Xanathar's Guide to Everything.

Artificers use the spell list from Eberron: Rising From the Last War.

Adventuring

Exploration and social interaction are described in the Player's Handbook as two of the three pillars of a Dungeons & Dragons adventure. This section describes house rules relevant to those aspects of the game.

Resting

Most players think of short rests as requiring 1 hour of downtime, and long rests as requiring 8 hours, although the Dungeon Master's Guide describes variant rules for resting. The house rule presented here is not equivalent to any of those variants.

Instead of differentiating short and long rests by duration, this rule differentiates them by the conditions that trigger them in gameplay.

A short rest is something that the players can take—unless the DM interrupts the rest or forbids it for some reason, players should assume that they can take a short rest in most situations, so long as there are no immediate distractions or demands.

By contrast, a long rest is something that the DM awards to the players. Players may not take a long rest until the DM says so, no matter how much time is available to their characters.

Of course, the DM should not withhold long rests without reason. As a guideline, the DM should award long rests when the party is between adventures and in a safe and secure location. The DM may well choose not to award long rests for many calendar days of game time, if the party is traveling through dangerous terrain for the duration or the party is otherwise under pressure.

At the DM's discretion, the party may long rest even in the middle of a dungeon or adventure, so long as the circumstances warrant it. For example, the party may discover an area that is guarded by powerful enchantments that prevent conflict, or they may otherwise secure an area well enough for a long enough time that the DM decides a long rest is well-deserved.

The effects of a short or long rest are as described in the base rules.

Why Limit Long Rests?

Dungeons & Dragons is designed to be a game of attrition and resource management. Some characters have fewer or weaker resources that recharge after a short rest, and others have more powerful resources, or a greater number, that recharge after a long rest. If the party takes a long rest in between each encounter, then the balance between these two types of resources is skewed; characters with more powerful long rest resources will shine, and those with less powerful short rest resources will struggle. Alternatively, those with many long rest resources will find that they never have the opportunity to use them, while those with few short rest resources will still be able to use them to their full extent on each encounter. As a design goal, a DM is intended to design adventures that are broken up into "adventuring days" of 4–6 encounters, with short rests in between and long rests only at the end of the "day".

However, with the rules as written in the Player's Handbook, certain types of adventures are very difficult to balance around the adventuring day concept. If the adventure spans many days of in-game calendar time, the characters are likely to have many long rests. This makes it hard to do wilderness exploration adventures. It can also be difficult to justify long rests in the middle of a dungeon, even though the dungeon is bigger than 4–6 encounters.

By limiting long rests to be available only when the DM grants them, it becomes possible to design adventures around the "adventuring day", even if that abstract "day" in fact takes many days, or just a few hours.
